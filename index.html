I work in microeconomic theory, especially at the intersection of strategic communication and search.

My current research asks how institutions shape learning in environments with misaligned experts. I am particularly interested in applications of information economics to technology regulation, encompassing social media, artificial intelligence, and algorithms.

Research agenda

Strategic information before disclosure.

A common assumption in information economics is that agents decide what to disclose after information is already available. My work instead emphasizes the prior stage: agents often choose how much evidence, expertise, or diagnostic information to acquire before deciding what to reveal.

Acquire → Select → Disclose → Infer

Work in progress

Disclosure with Cheap Evidence

Evidence is often selectively disclosed: agents can show what they found, but not necessarily what they chose not to find. This project studies how the cost of acquiring evidence affects credibility under discretionary disclosure.

The central question is whether cheaper evidence acquisition makes communication more informative, or instead raises the evidentiary standard so much that ordinary disclosures lose persuasive force.

Work in progress

Searching for Expertise

This project studies strategic communication when a receiver can search across experts whose preferences are uncertain.

The model asks when access to more advice disciplines biased experts, and when search instead changes the composition of experts from whom advice is informative.
