Research agenda

Strategic communication and search

My current research lies at the intersection of strategic communication and search. Much of the canonical literature asks how privately informed agents communicate once their information environment is fixed. I instead examine settings where agents can search for information too. What changes if we consider experts' incentives to acquire information, or decisionmakers' incentives to cross-examine experts, for instance? I am particularly interested in applications to technology governance, encompassing social media, artificial intelligence, and algorithms.

Work in progress

Disclosure with Cheap Evidence

When evidence is cheap, disclosure is governed by a sharp rule: full revelation arises if the quality of evidence exceeds a threshold, but no communication is possible otherwise. When evidence is weak, experts' incentive to acquire excess evidence harms their credibility. I apply this to AI evaluations and the labor market.

Work in progress

Searching for Expertise

When a decisionmaker can search across a pool of cheap-talking experts, lower search costs can harm learning by distorting communication. The temptation to search erodes the disciplinary effect that misaligned experts can have on each other. I apply this to social media.
